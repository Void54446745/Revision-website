Quote index

Macbeth
Start

“like Valour’s minion, carved out his passage”

Literally → Macbeth is described as the servant of bravery here.

Metaphorically → emphasises how, at the beginning of the play, S presents Macbeth not only as a courageous soldier, but also as someone who is happy to serve his king.

Verb “Carved” → Literally describes Macbeth violently killing everyone in his way.

Metaphorically → ‘carved’ is linked to artistry and precision, emphasising Macbeth’s skill on the battlefield.

Structural choice (we hear about Macbeth before we see him on stage)

Audience don’t see the battle on stage, or meet Macbeth himself straight away. Instead, we hear about his successes from a witness.

This emphasises Macbeth’s positive reputation, and establishes the world in which the play takes place as one in which men are celebrated for their mercilessness and violence in battle. Violence is celebrated if it is used to support the rightful King.

Middle

After murdering King Duncan in Act 2 Scene 2, Macbeth exclaims: "Will all great Neptune’s oceans wash this blood clean from my hands?”

Bloody hands = a symbol of Macbeth’s feelings of guilt as a result of committing the sin of regicide. This motif is used throughout the play to symbolise the inevitable consequences of sin.

Metaphor → emphasises the vast scale of Macbeth’s guilt, which he realises he will never be able to escape or make amends for.

Mythological reference to the Roman god of the sea → referencing a pagan god instead of the Christian god could be used to expose Macbeth’s deteriorating morality.

In Act 3 Scene 4, Macbeth attempts to escape his guilt by rejecting Banquo’s Ghost: [To Ghost] ‘Thou canst not say I did it! Ne’er shake thy gory locks at me’ [Seeing the Ghost] ‘Avaunt and quit my sight! Let the earth hide thee’

Ghost of Banquo = could be interpreted as a symbol of Macbeth’s guilty conscience.

Repetition of negatives (‘canst not’ ‘ne’er) → reveals Macbeth’s attempts to deny his involvement in Banquo’s murder and escape blame.

Imperative verbs (‘avaunt’ ‘quit’ ‘let’) → literally: Macbeth is ordering the Ghost of Banquo to leave. Metaphorically: exposes Macbeth’s refusal to face the consequences of his actions or take responsibility for his sins.

Exclamations → further expose Macbeth’s fear / his desperation to escape blame for the brutal murder of his friend, Banquo.

End

Macbeth: Seize upon Fife, give to th’ edge o’ th’ sword / His wife, his babes, and all unfortunate souls.

Imperative (command) ‘give’ → exposes Macbeth’s impulsive and vengeful reaction to the Witches’ warning about Macduff. He is thoughtless in his use of violence.

Listing → reveals Macbeth’s willingness to destroy anyone who stands in his way, even if they are innocent. He is not afraid to commit sin.

“Wife” and “babes” → symbols of innocence and vulnerability. Highlights Macbeth’s tyranny, as kings were expected to protect women and children, not attack them.

“Dead butcher and fiend-like queen”

Cyclical structure → S deliberately starts and ends his play with the killing of a traitor to expose how regicide will always have destructive consequences.

Macbeth + Lady Macbeth not referred to by name → used to highlight their total loss of respect and reputation by the end of the play.

Describing M as a “butcher” → literally: people who routinely kill and dismember living creatures. Metaphorically emphasises not only how many people Macbeth has ruthlessly murdered, but also his total desensitisation to violence.

The usurper’s cursed head

Cyclical structure → S deliberately starts and ends his play with the killing of a traitor to expose how usurping will lead to punisment on earth and in the afterlife

The witches
Witches are powerful

[Thunder and lightning] “Fair is foul, and foul is fair”

Pathetic fallacy (thunder and lightning) → this immediately creates an ominous atmosphere, reinforcing Jacobean beliefs in the witches’ ability to control the weather.

Shakespeare deliberately structures his play by opening with the witches to emphasise the power of supernatural forces.

“And you all know, security Is mortals’ chiefest enemy”

Literally → Hecate says that humans are most vulnerable when they believe they are invincible/safe

As you all know -> creates a conspiratorial tone, revealing not only how the witches are omniscient, but also how they work together to strategically bring about Macbeth's downfall. arguably, they play a role in his downfall here

Juxtaposition between security and enemy -> further reinforces the witches all knowing powers: they know human nature well and are able to use Macbeth's weakness against him.

This reinforces the Jacobean belief witches put curses on good men to bring them down

Macbeth weak willed

“Stay you imperfect speakers, tell me more” vs “You should be women, and yet your beards forbid me to interpret that ye are so”

"Imperfect" -> Macbeth acknowledges the prophesies are incomplete or unclear, yet chooses to believe them anyway

Imperatives “stay” and “tell me” → expose how Macbeth is immediately interested in the witches’ prophecies, which could imply that he believes them.

CONTRAST Banquo’s reaction → he is immediately sceptical of the witches/doesn’t trust them. S uses the contrast in these reactions to expose that even though the witches are powerful, it is possible to avoid their manipulations.

Macbeth responsible for his actions

“I am stepp’d in blood so far that to return would be as tedious as to go’er”

Literally → he has committed so many sins, that it is pointless to try to behave morally again.

Metaphorically → this grotesque imagery of Macbeth standing in a pool of blood highlights the vast extent of his sins, and how many people he has killed (directly and indirectly).

“Tedious” (dull, boring) → creates a dismissive tone, exposing how Macbeth recognises the immorality of his actions, but he chooses to ‘commit to the course of evil’ (AC Bradley).

Motif of blood continued → except Macbeth chooses to ignore the guilt he associates with blood.

“How now you secret, black, and midnight hags? … I conjure you… Answer me”

Adjectives “secret” “black” and “midnight” → Macbeth acknowledges the witches' evil nature, yet chooses to associate with them anyway.

Imperatives -> Exposes Macbeths arrogance, believing he can control the supernatural.

Seize upon Fife, give to th’ edge o’ th’ sword / His wife, his babes, and all unfortunate souls.

Imperative (command) ‘give’ → exposes Macbeth’s impulsive and vengeful reaction to the Witches’ warning about Macduff. He is thoughtless in his use of violence.

The witches never told him to do that - he's commiting evil sins by choice

Power
IDEA 1:Duncan is the ideal king, chosen by god

"So meek…so clear in his great office that his virtues will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against the deep damnation of his taking off”

Literally - Macbeth is saying that if he kills Duncan, all of Duncan’s virtuous characteristics will scream out like angels against his unlawful murder

‘So clear’ = Duncan rules with clarity and vision - people buy into this and respect him, unlike with Macbeth.

“Plead like angels” → personification of his virtues as pleading angels, Duncan does not deserve to die, associated with heavenly goodness and purity

“Trumpet-tongued” → underlines the intensity of his positive qualities, will be sung and praised loudly and proudly

IDEA 2: Personal consequences of usurping power

After murdering King Duncan in Act 2 Scene 2, Macbeth exclaims: "Will all great Neptune’s oceans wash this blood clean from my hands?”

Bloody hands = a symbol of Macbeth’s feelings of guilt as a result of committing the sin of regicide. This motif is used throughout the play to symbolise the inevitable consequences of sin.

Exposes Macbeth's realisation he'll face punishment on earth and in the afterlife for his sins

Mythological reference to the Roman god of the sea → Macbeth is unable to reference the chtistian god after forsaking christian moral princaples by comitting the sin of regicide

‘All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand’

Hyperbole → emphasises Lady Macbeth’s desperation to escape her feelings of guilt but also highlights her realisation that she will face the consequences of her regicide in the afterlife (contrast - ‘a little water clears us of this deed’).

S uses Lady Macbeth’s transformation from a callous and remorseless manipulator, into a vulnerable woman who feels immense guilt for her actions, to expose the inevitable personal consequences of committing regicide.

Diminutive “little” → exposes Lady Macbeth’s striking transformation into a vulnerable, weak woman - her regicide made her infinitely weaker, instead of gaining her power

IDEA 3: Wider consequences of tyranny

‘The minions of their race…turn’d wild in nature…as they would make war with mankind … Tis said they eat each other’

Noun “minions” → exposes the horses devotion and loyalty to Duncan.

Grade 7 AO2: deliberate mirroring of language here with the way Macbeth as described by the Captain (‘Valour’s minion’). The horses rebellion against their human masters reflects Macbeth’s challenge to The Great Chain of Being when he murders Duncan.

Contrast adjective “wild” → emphasises how the regicide creates severe disruption to the natural order, as the once-loyal horses now rebel against their human masters (reflecting the way Macbeth rebelled against Duncan)

Further emphasised by the reference to cannibalism (the ultimate unnatural action)

Grade 7 AO3: Shakespeare clearly engages with the ‘body politic’ theory of kingship here, revealing how what happens to the king’s natural body will have devastating consequences of the political body of Scotland, extending the brutality and selfishness of Macbeth’s actions.

‘I think our country sinks beneath the yoke. It weeps, it bleeds, and each new day a gash / Is added to her wounds.’

Metaphor “sinks beneath the yoke” → exposes the oppression that the people of Scotland suffer under Macbeth[‘s tyrannical rule.

Gruesome/violent imagery “weeps” “bleeds” “gash” “wounds” → reveals the grave extent of harm and pain that Macbeth’s usurpation has wrought on Scotland.

Personification → Scotland is personified as a vulnerable female victim - heightens Macbeth’s villainy and his indiscriminate use of violence and force to maintain his power.

Grade 7 AO3: By invoking ideas of the ‘body politic’, Shakespeare forcefully condemns regicide as it not only corrupts an individual’s soul but also destroys the very fibre of a nation.

IDEA 4: Usurpers will always be punished and lose power

‘His title hang loose about him, like a giant’s robe upon a dwarfish thief’

Simile comparing the ‘title’ of King to a ‘giant’s robe’ on a ‘dwarfish thief’ → emphasises how Macbeth is not only unworthy of the title of King as a result of his mercilessness and tyranny, he was NEVER worthy of the title because he committed regicide.

Contrast ‘giant’ vs. ‘dwarfish’ → emphasises Macbeth’s total loss of reputation by the end of the play (contrast ‘For Brave Macbeth - well he deserves that name’)

Comparison of Macbeth to a ‘thief’ - link to ‘cursed usurper’ → exposes how Macbeth’s position as King is unnatural and a disruption of the natural order because he challenges the Divine Right of Kings.

Lady Macbeth
Beginning - Lady Macbeth is ambitious, powerful and transgressive

Come you spirits, unsex me here…Come to my woman's breasts, And take my milk for gall

Repetition of imperative “come” → emphasises Lady Macbeth’s evil nature: she is unafraid to call on dark supernatural spirits. Challenges Jacobean Christianity.

Emphasises the way Lady Macbeth transgresses expectations of women as nurturing, maternal figures = rejects her feminine identity.

Plucked my nipple from its boneless gums and dash’d the brains out

Literally → She would have killed her baby if she'd promised to

Violent verbs → exposes how lady Macbeth is willing to harm or kill innocents

Violent verbs → exposes how callous Lady Macbeth is, as these graphic images highlight her capacity for cruelty against innocent people.

Shows how Lady Macbeth subverts Jacobean expectations of women as nurturing and caring mothers.

When you durst do it, then you were a man.

“When…then” → this phrase reveals Lady Macbeth’s strategic emasculation of Macbeth: she manipulates him by undermining his masculinity.

“Durst” (dare) → associated with bravery and courage.

Exposes Lady Macbeth’s warped and transgressive view of what it means to be a man = she believes men should use violence (no matter how sinful) to get what they want.

later - Lady Macbeth is sidelined as Macbeth becomes paranoid

“Nought’s had all’s spent, ‘tis safer to be that which we destroy, than in destruction dwell in doubtful joy”

Literally → Lady Macbeth says that it would be safer to be dead like Duncan, than alive and feeling scared/paranoid about getting caught. Shows she regrets the murder.

Juxtaposition between danger/threat vs. safety and happiness → emphasises the obvious change in Lady M’s character: she is beginning to regret the regicide.

Macbeth tells her: “Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck”

Imperative → exposes how Macbeth is becoming more assertive in his marriage to Lady M.

He orders her to pretend she knows nothing about his plan to kill Banquo. STARK CONTRAST to the beginning where she planned Duncan’s murder (‘leave all the rest to me’)

Term of endearment “dearest chuck” → shows how M reduces her to a submissive wife (contrast when he called her ‘my dearest partner in greatness’ in his letter in A1S5)

End - Lady Macbeth is powerless, going mad and committing suicide

‘All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand’

Hyperbole → emphasises Lady Macbeth’s desperation to escape her feelings of guilt but also highlights her realisation that she will face the consequences of her sins in the afterlife (contrast - ‘a little water clears us of this deed’).

S uses Lady Macbeth’s transformation from a callous and remorseless manipulator, into a vulnerable woman who feels immense guilt for her actions, to expose the inevitable consequences of committing sin.

Diminutive “little” → exposes Lady Macbeth’s striking transformation into a vulnerable, weak woman.

Offstage death: ‘She should have died hereafter’

Offstage death → S deliberately chooses for Lady Macbeth to die offstage = no one mourns her death - even M dismisses its importance (non-event). Jacobean audience would have seen this offstage death as a just punishment for her transgressions of The Great Chain of Being.

Direct contrast → Lady Macduff’s gory death takes place onstage = sympathetic audience response. Lady Macduff is celebrated for conforming to Jacobean expectations of women.
